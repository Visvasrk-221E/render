Introduction

Ecology


Ecology is the branch of science which deals with the relationships among different organisms and thier populations, their physical environment and biological surroundings.

[1] Levels in which ecology is focused :
Ecology mainly focuses on the four levels.

  • Organisms
  • Populations
  • Communities
  • Biomes

[2] Habitat :
The place where an organism live and interact with it's environment is called its habitat.

Organisms Debrief

What is an organism?


  • Every Individual of a species is called as an organism
  • Organisms which naturally interbreed among themselves and produce a fertile offspring constitues a population
  • At organim level, ecology is termed as physiological ecology where it deals with the adaptations observed in differenc organisms.
  • Adaptations are necessary for a population of organisms to survive and reproduce within their own habitats.

What is a population?


  • Individuals of the same species in a given specific location and specific time constitue a population.
  • The individuals of the population are always subjected to the competition for basic needs such as food, water and shelter. Such a competition is known as Intraspecific Competition

What is a community?


  • A biological community is constituted by an assemblage of populations of different species that live in an area and interact among themselves.
  • A biotic community of any area has a peculiar species composition and structure.

What are biomes?


A biome is a large unit, constituting of major vegetation type and associated with different flora and fauna in that specific climate zone.
Major biomes of India includes:

  • Tropical forests
  • Decidious forests
  • Sea coasts
  • Deserts, etc

Populations

Population Attributes


Population ecology is the link between the population, genetics and evolution.
Natural Selection is a process for the survival of the fittest and it is established in population level, ie in each population.
A population always has certain attributes (characteristics that are not found in individual organisms such as..)

  • Birth Rate(Natality)
  • Death Rate(Mortality)
  • Sex Ratio
  • Population Density
  • Population Pyramid, etc.

Population


A population is composed of individuals that belong different age group and same species at a specific time.
The three ecological age groups are :

  • Pre-reproductive age group : Those groups that has not yet achived the reproductive capability. They are often placed in the base of a population pyramid.
  • Reproductive age group : These are the members of population which has the ability to readily reproduce and give a healthy offspring. They come next to the pre-rep group
  • Post reproductive age group : These are members how have lost the capacity to reproduce and are on the top of the population pyramid.

If the age distribution, ie the number of individuals in the different age groups are plotted, it results in an age pyramid.
The structure of age pyramid shows whether the population is declining, growing or stable.

Population Growth


[1] Size of Population : The size of any population depends on many factors and is never the same. It keeps on changing with respect to time. The factors which affect the population are :

  • Food avaliablity
  • Predation Pressure
  • Weather

[2] Population Density : The change in population density of a population in a given habitat during a given time interval is due to the four basic factors or processes :
  • Natality
    Refers to the number of births during a given period in a population that are added to the initial density.
  • Mortality
    It is the number of deaths in the population during a given period.
  • Immigration
    It is the number of individuals of the same species that have come into the habitat from elsewhere during the time period under consideration.
  • Emmigration
    It is the number of individuals of the population whho left the habitat and gone elsewhere during the time period under consideration.
Natality and immigration always contribute to the increase in the population density, wheras the factors Mortallity and Emmigraton decreases the population density.

Some Basic Definitions

Population
The organisms belonging to the same species which naturally interbreed among themselves to produce a fertile offspring, and located in a specific region in a given time constitues a population.

Population Density
The number of individuals which belong to same species that occupy per unit area in a given time is called as population density, often expressed in terms of sq.metre or sq.km

Birth Rate
The per capita births in a population per unit time is known as birth rate. It tells us how fast new individuals are added to the new population. It is expressed as the ratio of increase in population to the initial population. Say there are 20 lotus in a pond initially and the increase in population is 8 per week, then the birth rate is 8/20 = 0.4 per week.

Death Rate
The per capita number of deaths in a population per unit time is defined as Mortality or death rate. It quantifies the loss of individuals due to death. Say there was initially about 40 bees in a lab. Due to some env conditions, 4 of them died of starvation in one week, then the death rate can be defined as the ratio of loss to the initial population in a given time interval(note, not 40-4 = 36, which is population after the loss), ie 4/40 = 0.1 per week.

Sex Ratio
It is defined as the proportion of males and females in a population; in human demograpy it is always expressed in terms of number of females per 1000 males. It influences reproductive potential and growth because mating dynamics depend on the availability of both sexes.

Population Pyramid/Age Pyramid
Graphical representation of a population's age structure (pre-reproductive, rep, post), often plotted seperately for males and females, sometimes combined with sex ratio. Its shape(pyramidal/expanding, bell/stationary, urn/declining) indicates whether the population is growing, stable or shrinking.

Predation Pressure
The intensity of predation acting on a prey population, ie the mortailty risk imposed by predators over time and space is called as predation pressure. Higher predation pressure reduces prey abundance and can drive behavioural, morphological and life-history adaptations, influencing community stability.

Population GrowthModels

What are population growth models?


Human beings have been concerned about the unbridled human population growth and problems created by it in our country and it is therfore natural for us to be curious if different animal populations in natre behave the same way or show some restraints on growth. Perhaps, we can learn a lesson or two from nature on how to control population growth.

Hence forth, it is necessary for us to develop a coherent mathematical model which can input data as population attributes and show output on how it is likely to change in the near future.

Such models to make observation on population, predict the forthcomming population and any history of records are called as population growth models.

Two types of population growth models.


  • Exponential Growth model wich is on the basis that ther is unlimitted food and resource in the habitat and there is zero intra or interspecific competition, ie, the organisms do not starve each other to death by competition.
  • Logistic growth model, which is based on the principle that there are only limitted resources and operates till a carrying capacity, the maximum population that the habitat can withstand, ie it is a environmental constraint specific to the habitat.


[A] Exponential Growth Model

  • This growth model follows an exponential rate of growth, gemometric fashion.
  • The rate of change of the population density is proportional to the initial population and the intrinsic rate of natural increase.
  • The intrinsic rate of natural increase is an important parameter to access the impacts of biotic or abiotic factors on the growth of population. It is expressed as r (r = b - d), ie it is the difference between the natality and mortality rates.
  • Wen a popoulation shows exponential growth, the curve plotted with N with respect to time t assumes J shaped pattern.


[1] Equations for EGM

  • N(t) = N0ert : This equation provides the population density for a given time t.
  • N0 : It is the initial population density at time t = 0.
  • r : Intrinsic rate of natural increase.
  • e : euler's base of natural logarithm(2.7181...)

[2] Dependencies with the r value

Parameter Value Meaning of Growth
r>0 Population grows rapidly
r = 0 Population is stationary, ie no growth
r<0 Population decays rapidly, exponential decay

[3] Graphical Review

Shape of Curve : J Shaped
Initial Phase : Lag Phase, slow and steady
Second Phase : Exponential Phase, blazing fast growth.

[4] Real Life Example

Bacteria, under lab conditions if perfect nutrition medium is provide, exhibits exponential growth for the initial amount of time. Binary fission causes the population to double every 20 minutes in case of E.coli. This happens untill intraspecific competition occurs.

[5] Assumptions in this Model

  • Unlimitted Resources(ideal)
  • No disease, predators, or space limitation occurs
  • Constant environment, no changes in the habitat
  • Instantaneous reproductoin.
  • It is a clean room stimulation perfect for theory, but rarely happens in real world for long time.

[6] Limitations

  • Ignores K, predicts infinite growth
  • Ignores death by competition, unrealistic over long periods.
  • No resource depletion, not applicable to ecosystmes in long term.

[B] Pearl-Verhulst Logistic Growth Model

  • Organisms(humans, animals, bactierias, etc) can not be growing forever. Resources like food, space, water, will run out.
  • So, unlike exponential growth, which assumes unlimitted everything, the logistic growth model shows how growth slows down as the population gets closer to a limit known as carrying capacity(K).
  • A population growing in a habitat with limitted resources shows initially a lag phase, followed by phases of accleration and finally and asymptote, when the population density reaches the carrying capacity.


[1] Equations

  • dN/dt = N0r(1 - N0/K)
  • Where r is the intrinsic rate of natural increase
  • N = popoulation or population density at a given time
  • K is the carrying capacity, the maximum the environment can support.

Life History Variations

Reason for evolution


Populations evolve to maximise thier reproductive fitness, also called as Darwinian fitness. Species with darwinian fitness always has high r value, in the habitat in which they live. Under a particular set of selection pressures, organisms evolve towards the most effective reproductive stage

Breeding Strategies


Some organisms breed only once in thier lifetime(most birds and mammals), some produce a large number of small sided offspring(Oysters, pelagic fishes) while others produce a small number of large sized offspring(birds, mammals). So the optimise towards how to maximise thier reproductive fitness.

Evolutionary Relationships


Some ecologists suggest that life history traits of organisms have evolved in relation to the constraints imposed by the abiotic and biotic components of the habitat in which they live in. Evolution of life history traits in different species is currently important area of research being conduced by ecologists.

Case Study : Is Sexual Deceit a 'real mutualism'?


Skip this question. Ncert says that it is mutualism. (only for those who thinks out of system)

No!. Ophrys 'sexual deceit' is not a mutualism; it is pollination by deception in which male bees are lured by female-mimicking sights and scents, but receive no reward such as nectar or a mating opportunity. The bee typically gains no benefit and can even incur costs like wasted time and ejaculate during pseudocopulation, so the interaction is exploitative rather than mutualistic. Mutualism requires benefits to both partners, but sexually deceptive orchids provide no food or mating payoff to thier pollinators, so the pollinator lacks a benefit. Given the absense of pollinator reward and the presense of deception, the relationship is viewed as sensory exploitation/antagonism, not a mutualism! It is antagonistic(similar to parasitism or predation, one is harmed, another is benifits).

Predation

Definition


Predation is an interspecific interaction where one animal (predator kills) the other animal,ie it's food (prey). In this relation, one species is benefitted, other is harmed.

Predation as process for energy transfer


Predation is part of the food chain where the energy fiexed by plants is transferred to the higher trophic levels, for example the tiger eating deer, snake the frog, sparrow the insect.

Role of predators in ecosystem


  1. They act as a channel for energy transfer from lower to higher trophic leverls.
  2. They keep the population of the preys in control, whereas otherwise, they will reach very high population density and disturb the ecosystem.
  3. Species diversity in a community is maintained by reducing the intensity of competition among the competing prey species.

Predation defences and overexploitation


If a predator is too efficient and overexploits its prey, then the prey might become extinct and following it, the predator will also get extincet for lack of food. This is the reason why predators in nature are prudent. Prey species have evolved various defenses to lessen the impact of predation.Some of the methods are given below.

Camouflage(cryptic coloration)

It is observed in certain insect species and frogs to avoid detection by their predators, eg vietnamese mossy frog, the leaf mantis green chameleon camouflaged by taking colors.


Distastefulness

Some animals are highly distasteful to thier predators, so are saved from being devoured by the predator. The monarch butterfly species accumulates a chemical compund by feeding on a poisonous weed (colotrophis) during its catepillar stage, and hence contributes towards its distastefullness.


Poison and toxin production

Some prey are poisonous and hence are avoided by predators, eg Dart frogs, like phyllobates bicolour, dendrobates, etc.


Herbivores are predators of plants


Phytophagus Organisms - Approximately, 25% of the known insects are phytophagous, ie they feed on the sap and other parts of plants. In order to evade from them, the plants have developed immense survival and defence mechanisms, such as following.

Parasitism

Considering that the parasitic mode of life ensures free lodging and meals, it is not surprising that parasitism has evolved in so many taxonomic groups from plants to higher vertebrates.Many parasites have evolved to be host specific, meaning, they can parasitise only a single species of host in sucah a way that both host and the parasite tend to co evolve. That is, if the host evolves special mechanisms for rejecting ro resisting the parasite, then the parasite has to evolve mechanisms to counteract and neutralise them, in order to be competant with the same host species.

Competition

Darwinian Competition and Modern Views


When Darwin spoke of the struggle for existence and survival of the fittest, he was convinced that the interspecific competitionis a potent force in organinc evolution. It is generally believed that competition occurs when closely related species compete for the same resources that are limiting, but this is not entirely true.There are two modifications on the basis of competition, build on top of the Darwinian principles. They are as shown below.


Firstly..

Totally unrelated species could also compete for the same resources. For einstance, in some shallow south american lakes, visiting flamingoes and resident fishes compete for their common food, the zooplankton in the lake.

Secondly..

Resources need not to be limitting for competition to occur, in interference competition, the feeding efficiency of one species might be reduced due to the interfering and inhibitory presence of the other species, even if the resources are abundant.


Definition of Competition


Competition is best defined as a process in which the fitness of one species(measured in terms of its r) is significantly lower in the presence of another species.There exists various evidences which showcases the occurance of competition among the organisms. Some principles are listed below


[A] Gause Competitive Exculsion Principle

This states that the two closely related species competing for the same resouces cannont co-exist indefinitely and the competitively inferior one will be eliminated eventually. This may be true if the resoures are limitting, but not otherwise.

[1] Abingdon Tortoise --Galapagos Islands

The abingdon tortoise of the galapagos islands became extinct within a decade after goats were introduced on the island, apparently due to greater-browising efficiency of the goats.

[2] Connell's elegant field experiment : Balanus Barnacle --coasts of scott

Connell's elegant field experiments showed that on the rocky sea coasts of scott, the larger and competitively superior barnacle Balanus dominates the intertidal coast zone, and excludes the smaller barnacle Chatamalus from that zone.


[B] Competitive Release

Another evidence for the occurrence of competition in nature comes from what is called as competitive release. A species whose distribution is restricted to a small geographical area because of the presence of a competitively small geographical area because of the presence of a competitively superior species, is found to expand its distributional range dramatically when the competing species is experimentally removed.


[C] Resource Partitioning

While this phenomenon opposes the gause principle, they do not rule out the occurrence of interspecific competition in nature, they point out that species facing competition might evolve mechanisms that promote co existing rather than exclusion. Once such mechanism is Resouce partitioning. One example is given below.

[1] The Warbler Species --in a Tree

If two species compete for the same resouce, they could avoid competition by choosing, for instance different times for feeding or different foraging patterns. Mac Arthur showed that five closely related species of warblers living on the same tree were able to avoid competion and co exist due to behavioural differences in their foraging activites.


General Section

Why does herbivores and plants appear to be more adversly affected by competition that carnivores?

As per NCERT, herbivores and plants appear to be more adversely affected by competition than carnivores because herbivores depend directly on plants, which are limited and stationary resources. Plants compete intensely with each other for sunlight, nutrients, and space, and herbivores compete for this limited plant biomass as their sole food source. In contrast, carnivores often have a broader range of prey and can switch between different prey species, reducing their direct competition. Additionally, plants cannot escape herbivory and must develop various defenses, leading to stronger competitive pressures on both plants and herbivores. Thus, the limited and specialized food resources make herbivores and plants more vulnerable to adverse effects of competition compared to carnivores.
