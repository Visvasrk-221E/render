Introduction

What are the two types of nucleic acids? How are they used?


Deoxyribonucleic acid and ribonucleic acid (DNA and RNA) respectively are the two types of nucleic acids found in living systems.

DNA acts as a genetic material in most organisms. RNA though it also acts as a genetic material in some viruses, mostly functions as a messanger. RNA has additional roles as well. RNA has a function as adapter, structural and in some case as a catalytic molecule.

What are nitrogen bases?


Nitrogen bases are nitrogen compounds that form an essential part of nucleotides, the building blocks of the nucleic acids. They are heterocyclic aromatic compounds, that is they have rings containing both carbon and nitrogen atoms. These are of two types : Purines and Pyrimidines.Purines usually have 6 membered pyrimidine ring fused with 5 membered imidazole ring.

Structure of DNA

Nitrogen Bases : Build up of Nucleic Acids


The nitrogen bases in the nucleic acids are of two types. Purines and Pyrimidines. Purines are double ringed structure with a six membered ring fused with a five member ring. That is pyrimidine + imidazole ring is purine. The nitrogen base derivatives of purines are Adinine and Guanine. Wheras pyrimidines are six membered ring structure whose derivative nitrogen bases are Thymine, Uracil, Cytozine.

What is a nucleoside and nucleotide?


Nucleoside is a biomolecule that consists of a nitrogenous base (either purine/pyrimidine) linked to sugar molecule (either ribose or deoxyribose). Examples are Adenosine, guanosine, thymidine, cytidine, uridine.

If a phosphate group is also found to be esterified with a nucleoside, then it is known as nucleotide, the basic monomer of nucleic acids. Examples are Adenylic acid, thymidylic acid, guanylic acid, uridylic acid, cytidylic acid.

Structure of DNA


A nitrogen base is linked to the hydroxyl group (-OH) of the 1'C (Carbon) of pentose sugar through N-glycosidic linkage to form a nucleoside. A phosphage group is linked to (-OH) of the 5'C of a nucleoside trhough phosphoester linkage to form a nucleotide. The nucleotides are linked through 3'-5' phosphodiester linkage to form a polynucleotide chain whcih has 5'-end formed by a free phosphate moiety and a 3'-end formed by a free (-OH) of 3'C group of the sugar. Therefore the 5'-end has phosphate end, 3'-end has free hydroxy end.

The pentose sugar and phosphate group form the backbone of the polynucleotide chain form which nitrogen bases project. The base pairing pattern between the two polynucleotide chains showed that they are complementary to each other. Thus if the sequence of bases in one strand is knwon then one can predict the sequence in another strand. It was also inferred that, if each strand from a DNA (parent DNA) acts as a template for synthesis of a new daughter strand, the two double stranded DNA, thus produced, would be identical to the parental DNA.

Salient Features of DNA

What is DNA made up of?


DNA is made up of two polynucleotide chains, where the backbone is made by pentose sugar, phosphate group, and the nitrogenous bases project inside .

Provide the description of DNA


  • DNA is negatively charged due to the presense of phophate groups in its backbone.
  • The nitrogen bases in the two polynucleotide strands are paired through hydrogen bonds (H-bonds) forming base pairs.
  • Adenine of one strand forms two hydrogen bonds with thymine of opposite strand and vice versa (A double bond T). Guanine of one strand forms three hydrogen bonds with cytosine of the opposite strand(G tripple bond C).
  • As a result of complementary base pairing, always purine comes opposite to a pyrimidine. This generates uniform distance between the two strands of DNA helix. Diameter of DNA is 2 nm(nanometer).
  • The two chains have antiparallel polarity because of the pairing between purines and pyrimidines i.e one chains has 5' to 3' polarity, other has 3' to 5' polarity.


Salient Features of DNA

  • The two chains are coiled in a right handed helical fashion.
  • This coiling produces minor and major grooves.
  • There are 10bp in each turn.
  • Distance between two adjacent bp is 0.34 nm
  • The plate of one nitrogenous base pair stacks over the other on top of each other in a double helix. This in addition to H bonds confers to stability of the helical structure.


The Central Dogma

The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology is a fundamental framework describing the flow of genetic information within a biological system. It states that genetic information flows in one direction from DNA to RNA to protein. DNA serves as the template for its own replication and is transcribed into RNA, which in turn is translated into protiens, the functional molecules responsible for cellular structure and function. The central dogma consists of the following processes :

  • Replication : DNA makes an exact copy of itself to pass genetic information to daughter cells.
  • Transcription : The information encoded in a DNA strand is copied into messenger RNA(mRNA) by the enzyme RNA polymerase.
  • Translation : The mRNA sequence is decoded by ribosomes to synthesize proteins by assembling amino acids in the correct order.

This theory was first formulated by Francis Crick in 1958, who emphasized that information cannot flow back from protein to nucleic acid, although exceptions exist (such as reverse transcription in retroviruses and Non coding RNA functions, the Central Dogma remains a core principle explaining how genetic informatoin directs cellular functions.

Length of DNA


The length of DNA double helix in a typical mammalian cell is approximately 2.2 meters (total number of bp i.e 6.6 x 10^9 bp x distance between two adjacent base pairs i.e 0.34 x 10^(-9)m . Therefore the length of DNA for a specified number of base pairs is given by the product of number of base pairs and the distance between two base pairs.

DNA packing in prokaryotes


In prokaryotes, due to the absense of a defined nucleus, the DNA present is in the cytoplasm. But the prokaryotic DNA is not scattered throughout the cytoplasm, but it is supercoiled with the help of RNAs and some positively charged protiens that are non histone proteins like polyamines to form a compact space of mass called nucleoid.
